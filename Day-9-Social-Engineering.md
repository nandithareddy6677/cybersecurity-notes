# 📖 Introduction

Not every cyber attack targets computers directly. Many attackers target people instead.

Humans are often considered the weakest link in cybersecurity because they can be manipulated into revealing confidential information or performing actions that compromise security.

This type of attack is known as **Social Engineering**.

Instead of exploiting software vulnerabilities, attackers exploit **human trust, curiosity, fear, or emotions**.

---

# 🤝 What is Social Engineering?

Social Engineering is the psychological manipulation of people to perform actions or reveal confidential information.

Attackers often pretend to be:

- Bank officials
- Company employees
- IT Support staff
- Government officers
- Delivery agents
- Friends or colleagues

Their goal is to steal:

- Passwords
- OTPs
- Banking information
- Personal data
- Company secrets

---

# Why is Social Engineering Dangerous?

Social Engineering attacks are dangerous because:

- They bypass technical security controls.
- They exploit human behavior.
- They are difficult to detect.
- Even well-secured systems can be compromised if users are deceived.

---

# Common Types of Social Engineering Attacks

## 1. Phishing

Phishing is the most common Social Engineering attack.

Attackers send fake emails pretending to be trusted organizations.

Purpose:

- Steal passwords
- Collect banking information
- Install malware

Example:

A fake email claiming to be from your bank asking you to verify your account.

---

## 2. Spear Phishing

Spear Phishing is a targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization.

Unlike regular phishing, the attacker researches the victim before sending the email.

Example:

An attacker sends an email addressed specifically to a company's finance manager.

---

## 3. Whaling

Whaling targets high-profile individuals such as:

- CEOs
- Directors
- Executives
- Government officials

These attacks often involve financial fraud or confidential company information.

---

## 4. Smishing

Smishing is phishing conducted through SMS (text messages).

Example:

"You have won ₹10,00,000. Click here to claim your prize."

---

## 5. Vishing

Vishing (Voice Phishing) uses phone calls to trick victims.

Example:

An attacker pretends to be a bank employee asking for your OTP.

---

## 6. Baiting

Attackers lure victims by offering something attractive.

Examples:

- Free software
- Free movie downloads
- Infected USB drives labeled "Salary Report"

The victim unknowingly installs malware.

---

## 7. Pretexting

The attacker creates a fake story or scenario to gain trust.

Example:

Pretending to be an IT administrator asking for login credentials.

---

## 8. Tailgating (Piggybacking)

An unauthorized person follows an authorized employee into a secure area.

Example:

Someone carrying boxes asks an employee to hold the door open.

---

## 9. Dumpster Diving

Attackers search through discarded documents or electronic waste to obtain confidential information.

Examples:

- Password notes
- Financial records
- Employee information

---

# Real-Life Example

An employee receives an email appearing to come from the company's IT department.

The email requests the employee to reset their password using a provided link.

The employee clicks the link and enters their login credentials.

The attacker immediately gains access to the company's internal systems.

This is a classic phishing attack.

---

# Signs of a Social Engineering Attack

- Urgent or threatening messages.
- Requests for passwords or OTPs.
- Poor grammar or spelling.
- Unknown email addresses.
- Suspicious links.
- Unexpected attachments.
- Offers that seem too good to be true.

---

# Prevention Best Practices

- Never share passwords or OTPs.
- Verify the identity of unknown callers.
- Be cautious of urgent requests.
- Hover over links before clicking.
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
- Keep software updated.
- Attend cybersecurity awareness training.
- Report suspicious emails immediately.

---

# Organizational Security Measures

Organizations should:

- Conduct employee awareness training.
- Implement email filtering.
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication.
- Apply Least Privilege Access.
- Regularly perform phishing simulations.
- Monitor suspicious activities.

---

# 📌 Key Takeaways

- Social Engineering targets people rather than systems.
- Human error is often the weakest security point.
- Phishing is the most common Social Engineering attack.
- Spear Phishing targets specific individuals.
- Smishing uses SMS messages.
- Vishing uses phone calls.
- Tailgating provides unauthorized physical access.
- Security awareness is the best defense.
